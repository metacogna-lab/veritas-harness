## Abstract

Harnessing the power of human-annotated data through Supervised Fine-Tuning (SFT) is pivotal for advancing Large Language Models (LLMs). In this paper, we delve into the prospect of growing a strong LLM out of a weak one without the need for acquiring additional human-annotated data. We propose a new fine-tuning method called Self-Play fIne-tuNing (SPIN), which starts from a supervised fine-tuned model. At the heart of SPIN lies a self-play mechanism, where the LLM refines its capability by playing against instances of itself. More specifically, the LLM generates its own training data from its previous iterations, refining its policy by discerning these self-generated responses from those obtained from human-annotated data. Our method progressively elevates the LLM from a nascent model to a formidable one, unlocking the full potential of human-annotated demonstration data for SFT. Theoretically, we prove that the global optimum to the training objective function of our method is achieved only when the LLM policy aligns with the target data distribution. Empirically, we evaluate our method on several benchmark datasets including the HuggingFace Open LLM Leaderboard, MT-Bench, and datasets from Big-Bench. Our results show that SPIN can significantly improve the LLM's performance across a variety of benchmarks and even outperform models trained through direct preference optimization (DPO) supplemented with extra GPT-4 preference data. This sheds light on the promise of self-play, enabling the achievement of human-level performance in LLMs without the need for expert opponents.

## Summary

**Problem.** LLM alignment methods (SFT, RLHF, DPO) all require costly human-annotated preference data. The question is whether a weak LLM can improve itself using only the SFT dataset it was trained on, with no additional annotation.

**Approach.** SPIN frames fine-tuning as a two-player game. At iteration *t*, the "opponent" is the previous LLM version (pθ_t), which generates responses y′ to prompts from the SFT dataset. The "main player" (pθ_{t+1}) is trained to distinguish human responses *y* from the opponent's synthetic responses *y′*, using a logistic loss objective. The opponent is then replaced by pθ_{t+1} and the process repeats. No preference dataset, reward model, or external annotator is needed — only the original SFT dataset.

**Key results.**
- On zephyr-7b-sft-full (Mistral-7B base), SPIN improves HuggingFace Open LLM Leaderboard average from 58.14 → 63.16 (+5.02), with 10%+ gains on GSM8k and TruthfulQA.
- MT-Bench score improves from 5.94 → 6.78.
- Results are competitive with DPO trained on an extra 62k preference dataset.
- Iterative self-play consistently improves over standard continued SFT, which plateaus or regresses.

**Theory.** The authors prove (Theorem 5.2) that the SPIN training objective's global optimum is achieved if and only if pθ_t = p_data — i.e., the process stops only when the model has fully matched the target distribution. A second theorem (5.4, logistic loss case) characterises the update rule: pθ_{t+1}(y|x) ∝ pθ_t(y|x) · (p_data(y|x)/pθ_t(y|x))^{1/λ}.

**Relation to DPO.** SPIN's training objective resembles DPO with logistic loss, but differs in that SPIN generates its own negatives from a previous self-iteration rather than from a human-curated preference dataset, and naturally leads to an iterative procedure rather than single-pass training.

**Limitations.**
- Requires access to the original SFT dataset (prompts + ground-truth responses).
- Number of iterations and λ are hyperparameters requiring tuning.
- Evaluated primarily on zephyr-7b; broader model family validation is left to future work.
- Convergence proof relies on assumptions about the function class that may not hold in practice.

**Significance.** SPIN demonstrates that iterative self-improvement is achievable for LLMs using only existing SFT data, without any human feedback loop. The self-play framing connects LLM fine-tuning to GAN-style training and boosting theory, offering a principled explanation for why iterative training improves over static SFT.
