## Abstract

We posit that to achieve superhuman agents, future models require superhuman feedback in order to provide an adequate training signal. Current approaches commonly train reward models from human preferences, which may then be bottlenecked by human performance level, and secondly these separate frozen reward models cannot then learn to improve during LLM training. In this work, we study Self-Rewarding Language Models, where the language model itself is used via LLM-as-a-Judge prompting to provide its own rewards during training. We show that during Iterative DPO training that not only does instruction following ability improve, but also the ability to provide high-quality rewards to itself. Fine-tuning Llama 2 70B on three iterations of our approach yields a model that outperforms many existing systems on the AlpacaEval 2.0 leaderboard, including Claude 2, Gemini Pro, and GPT-4 0613. While there is much left still to explore, this work opens the door to the possibility of models that can continually improve in both axes.

## Summary

**Problem Statement**

Standard RLHF pipelines are bottlenecked in two ways: (1) the reward model is trained from human preferences, capping the quality ceiling at human performance; (2) the reward model is frozen during LLM training, so it cannot improve as the LLM improves. This paper asks whether a single model can serve as both the instruction-following LLM and the reward model, allowing both to co-evolve.

**Approach**

Self-Rewarding LMs combine two capabilities in one model:
1. **Instruction following**: generate high-quality responses to user prompts.
2. **Self-instruction creation + self-reward**: generate new training prompts, generate N candidate responses, and evaluate them via LLM-as-a-Judge prompting (a 5-point additive scoring rubric covering relevance, coverage, usefulness, clarity, and expertise).

The training procedure is iterative DPO:
- **M0**: base Llama 2 70B.
- **M1**: M0 fine-tuned on seed IFT (3200 Open Assistant examples) + EFT (1630 LLM-as-a-Judge examples) via SFT.
- **M2**: M1 trained on AIFT(M1) — preference pairs generated by M1 — via DPO.
- **M3**: M2 trained on AIFT(M2) via DPO.

At each step, winning/losing response pairs are constructed by taking the highest/lowest scoring responses from N=4 candidates. New prompts are generated via 8-shot Self-Instruct sampling from Llama 2-Chat 70B (fixed). 3,964 pairs were added for M2; 6,942 for M3.

**Key Results**

- Instruction following improves each iteration: M3 wins 62.5% of head-to-head comparisons against the SFT baseline (vs. 30.5% for M1).
- AlpacaEval 2.0 win rate over GPT-4-Turbo: 9.94% (M1) → 15.38% (M2) → 20.44% (M3). M3 surpasses Claude 2 (17.19%), Gemini Pro (16.85%), and GPT-4 0613 (15.76%).
- Reward modeling ability also improves: LLM-as-a-Judge pairwise accuracy with human preferences increases from 65.1% (SFT baseline) to 78.7% (M1) to 80.4% (M2) to 81.7% (M3).
- Human evaluation (50 instructions, 3 annotators each) confirms the automatic evaluation trend.
- MT-Bench overall score improves from 6.85 (SFT baseline) to 7.25 (M3), with larger gains in humanities, STEM, roleplay, writing, and extraction than in math and code.
- NLP benchmark performance (ARC, HellaSwag, GSM8K, MMLU, NQ) remains roughly stable across iterations — the "alignment tax" is minimal.
- The model generates progressively longer responses across iterations (avg. 1092 tokens for M1, 2552 for M3), which may partly inflate win rates.

**Limitations**

- Response length grows with each iteration; longer responses may score higher under automatic evaluation independent of quality.
- Gains in math and logical reasoning are small — the approach mainly lets models better utilise existing knowledge rather than acquire new reasoning skills.
- Seed data (Open Assistant) under-represents reasoning and coding tasks, limiting gains in those categories.
- Prompts are single-turn; MT-Bench multi-turn improvements may be underestimated.
- Only 3 iterations are explored; saturation dynamics are unclear.
- Prompt generation is still performed by a fixed external model (Llama 2-Chat 70B), so the loop is not fully autonomous.

**Significance**

Self-Rewarding LMs demonstrate that a model's reward signal can co-evolve with its capabilities, circumventing the fixed-reward-model bottleneck of standard RLHF. The result — a Llama 2 70B model surpassing proprietary models like Claude 2 and GPT-4 on a public leaderboard using only open-source seed data — is a strong empirical proof-of-concept for autonomous self-improvement through iterative DPO with self-generated preference data. This is a key stepping stone toward superhuman alignment without superhuman human annotators.
